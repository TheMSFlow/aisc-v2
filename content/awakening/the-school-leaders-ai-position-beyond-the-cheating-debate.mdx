Ask a room of school leaders what AI means for education and watch how fast the conversation becomes a conversation about cheating. Detection tools, rewritten integrity policies, whether the essay is dead, what to do about the take-home anything. Understandable: the cheating question arrived first, loudest, and attached to real incidents on real desks.

But notice what that framing did. It took the largest question to reach education in a generation, what does abundant machine intelligence mean for how we prepare young people, and shrank it into an enforcement problem. It handed the institution's AI position to the discipline office. A school's entire posture toward the technology reshaping its students' futures became: how do we catch them using it?

You cannot catch your way to a position. And the school leader who stays in the enforcement frame is answering a small question while the large ones, the ones parents, teachers, boards, and students are actually carrying, get answered by somebody else.

## The questions the cheating frame is drowning out

Sit with the constituencies of any school community and the real question list is much longer than integrity policy.

Teachers are quietly asking whether their profession is being automated, whether using AI for lesson planning is resourcefulness or corner-cutting, and why the institution has an opinion about students using it but none about staff. Parents are asking what their children should actually learn now, whether the school is preparing kids for the world that is coming or the one the curriculum was written for, and, increasingly pointedly, what the school's AI plan is. Boards are asking the governance version of the same thing. And the students, who [adopted the technology before any policy existed](/awakening/your-students-adopted-ai-before-your-policy-did), are asking the sharpest question of all, usually silently: will the adults here teach me to use this well, or just punish me for using it badly?

Every one of those questions lands on the leader's desk eventually. None of them can be answered from the enforcement frame.

## What a real position covers

A school leader's AI position is a set of answers you could deliver, without notes, to a parent evening, a staff meeting, and a board session, and have them hold up. It covers four grounds the cheating debate never reaches.

**Learning.** Where does AI genuinely serve learning in this school, and where does it hollow it out? The honest answer requires [the delegation distinction](/awakening/what-to-delegate-to-ai-and-what-to-never) applied to education's core: work whose value is the output can be assisted; work whose value is the struggle, the thinking the student builds by doing it, must be protected, deliberately, assignment by assignment. A school that can articulate which is which has an actual pedagogy for the AI era. A school that cannot has a detection subscription.

**Staff.** What is your position on teachers and administrators using AI: the planning, the drafting, the reporting, the communication load that burns your best people out? A school that frees ten hours of a teacher's administrative week has bought ten hours of teaching energy with no budget line. Silence here does not prevent staff adoption; it just [guarantees the adoption happens without standards](/awakening/ai-governance-the-risk-is-already-inside), unevenly, and with private guilt attached to what should be a professional skill.

**Preparation.** The graduates walking out of your school will enter workplaces where directing AI well is a baseline capability. What, concretely, will they have learned about that here? A school with an answer is making a promise to parents no ranking table captures. This is also where the integrity question finally gets a real answer: students learn honest use of powerful tools by being taught it, assessed on it, and shown it modeled, not by being screened for it.

**Standards.** The data lines (student information never enters unvetted tools), the accountability lines (a human answers for every consequential decision, always), and the transparency lines (families know where AI touches their children's education). Boards fund positions; they can only ask questions about postures.

> A detection policy tells your community what the school is against. Only a position tells them what the school is for.

## Why this belongs to the leader, personally

Here is the uncomfortable center of it: none of the four grounds can be delegated to a committee, because all four require the leader to have personal clarity first. A head of school whose own understanding of AI is borrowed, from headlines, from the loudest voice on staff, from the vendor at the conference, will run a school whose position is borrowed too, and borrowed positions collapse at the first hard parent question.

The leaders who are getting this right share one visible trait: they did their own work first. They can explain, plainly, what these tools are and are not. They have used them enough to direct them and to know where they fail. They have decided what they believe about learning in an AI era, and the school's policies grew from that belief instead of substituting for it.

That personal clarity is exactly what the AI Stakeholder Challenge builds, and school leaders may be the audience it serves most naturally: seven days from borrowed anxiety to an owned position, with the governance capstone landing on the ground education actually stands on: the mandate to educate, train, and prepare the next generation of capability. The cheating debate will still be there afterward. It will just finally be the right size: one policy inside a position, instead of the position itself.
