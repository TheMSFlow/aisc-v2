Somebody has asked you for your AI strategy this year. A board member, an investor, a partner, or the version of you that lies awake doing the asking. And if the question produced a vague answer followed by a vague feeling, you are in the majority.

Here is what makes the question feel impossible: it sounds like it has infinite answers. It does not. Underneath the noise, there are five positions a leader can take on AI, and only five. Once you can name them, the paralyzing question "what should I do about AI?" collapses into a much smaller one: "which of these five am I?"

## Why a position beats a strategy

A strategy document describes what an organization intends. A position describes where a leader stands: the specific, claimable ground they intend to be known for as AI reshapes their world. Positions are better than strategies for one reason: they are falsifiable. You either are the person your industry calls for judgment on AI decisions, or you are not. You either built the thing that removed a real friction, or you did not.

Vague ambitions cannot fail, which is exactly why they cannot succeed. The five positions below are concrete enough to fail, which makes each of them concrete enough to build.

## The five positions

**The Optimizer** improves speed, cost, and margin inside an existing system. This is the operator's position: taking the organization you already run and using AI to remove the drag your industry has learned to tolerate. The Optimizer's win looks like a cost structure competitors cannot match and a pace they cannot explain. It fits leaders whose world already trusts them to run things well.

**The Producer** builds a new product or service that solves a real friction point others feel. Not innovation for its own sake: a thing people pay for because it relieves a cost they were absorbing. The Producer's win looks like revenue that did not exist last year. It fits leaders who see a gap clearly and would rather build than advise.

**The Advisor** provides judgment, direction, and clarity to others navigating AI decisions. Every industry is full of people making expensive AI choices with no one trusted to sanity-check them. The Advisor becomes that person: the one whose judgment gets bought, not their labor. It fits leaders whose experience is deep enough that pattern recognition is their real product.

**The Educator** designs and delivers learning that builds AI capability in others at scale. The gap between people who can work with AI deliberately and people who cannot is wide, visible, and growing, and closing it is a service worlds will pay for. The Educator's win looks like a reputation as the one who made a whole team, congregation, or audience capable. It fits leaders who are already the ones others learn from.

**The Curator** researches, evaluates, and synthesizes AI knowledge into trusted intelligence. In a world drowning in claims, the person who reads everything, tests what matters, and reports honestly becomes indispensable. The Curator's win looks like being the source others check against the noise. It fits leaders whose credibility is their platform, which is why it is a natural fit for many creators.

## The mistake is trying to hold all five

Read the list again and you will feel the temptation: a little optimizing here, some advising there, maybe a course later. Resist it. A leader who tries to hold all five positions holds none of them, because a position only pays when your world can complete the sentence "when it comes to AI, they are the one who..."

Optionality feels safe and produces nothing. Every week spent keeping all five doors open is a week someone else spends walking through one of them. The leaders who compound are the ones who chose.

> You do not need the right answer to every AI question. You need one position you can defend, in a world that knows you hold it.

## How to tell which one is yours

Three questions, answered honestly, usually make the choice obvious:

- **What does your world already trust you for?** The strongest position extends existing trust rather than requiring new credentials. An operator becomes an Optimizer more naturally than an Educator. A teacher at heart was always going to educate.
- **Where is the friction you can see that others cannot?** Your experience is a map of where things break, stall, and quietly cost money. The position that lets you claim the friction you see most clearly is the one with the shortest path to value. If you have not named that ground yet, [the territory question](/awakening/territory-not-tools-the-ai-opportunity-for-leaders) is the place to start.
- **Which win would you actually defend?** Imagine each position's win landed. Which one would you fight to keep? A position you would not miss is a position you do not want.

Notice that "which AI tools do I like" appears nowhere in the three questions. Tools change monthly. Positions hold for years.

## A position is also what the people above you are waiting for

If you answer to a board, the position is what turns [the AI conversation your board expects](/awakening/the-briefing-your-board-expects-you-to-have-had) from an interrogation into a presentation. If you are earlier in your career, the position is what separates "person who uses AI" from "person who leads it" long before anyone hands you the title. Either way, the declaring is the point. An undeclared position is an opinion, and opinions do not compound.
