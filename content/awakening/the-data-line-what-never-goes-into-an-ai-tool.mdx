Somewhere in your organization this week, someone pasted something into an AI tool to save twenty minutes. A client email, a salary spreadsheet, a draft contract, a patient note, a set of numbers that have not been announced yet. They were not being reckless. They were being efficient, with a tool that feels like a private scratchpad and is not one.

You already govern lines like this everywhere else. There are things your people know not to say to a vendor, not to leave on a train, not to send to a personal inbox. Almost no organization has drawn the equivalent line for AI tools yet, and the tools arrived faster than the instincts did. This guide is for drawing it: deliberately, this week, in writing.

## Why the line exists

Two plain facts, no alarmism required.

First, when information goes into an AI tool, it leaves your control. Depending on the tool and its settings, it may be stored, reviewed to improve the service, or retained in ways you will never see. Consumer versions of these tools, the free accounts your team signed up for themselves, generally offer the weakest promises. You should assume anything pasted into one has been disclosed to an outside party, because contractually, it usually has been.

Second, the failure is silent. Data does not leak with a noise. There is no moment where the confidential board figure visibly escapes; there is only, months later, the question you cannot answer: "where has this information been?" For regulated worlds, finance, healthcare, anywhere client confidentiality is the product, that unanswerable question is itself the incident.

This is [the ungoverned-adoption problem](/awakening/ai-governance-the-risk-is-already-inside) at its sharpest edge: the risk is not a bad actor. It is a good employee, saving twenty minutes.

## Draw the line in three tiers

A workable data line is not a ban and not a lecture. It is three tiers, written in plain language, that anyone can apply in five seconds without asking permission.

**Never enters any AI tool.** The unconditional tier. Anything identifying a specific person's health, finances, or employment situation: patient information, salaries, performance issues. Client and customer data you hold under confidence or contract. Credentials, security details, legal matters in progress. Unannounced numbers and decisions: results, deals, restructures. If disclosure of it to a stranger would be an incident, it is in this tier, and no deadline moves it.

**Enters only approved tools.** Work that is internal but not radioactive: process documents, draft communications, planning material with the sensitive specifics removed. This tier is where most of the productivity lives, and it is conditional on the organization actually approving tools: business-grade versions, vetted settings, a real decision by someone accountable instead of a hundred personal free accounts.

**Free to use anywhere.** Anything already public or generic: published material, general research, drafting that contains nothing yours. This tier exists so the line has a green zone, because a line that only forbids gets ignored.

The tier test that makes it usable: **before pasting, ask who could read this without it being a problem.** Anyone? Tier three. Colleagues? Tier two, approved tools only. Nobody outside a named circle? It never goes in.

<Callout label="The de-identification habit">
Much tier-one work becomes tier-two work with sixty seconds of effort: strip the names, replace the numbers with placeholders, describe the situation generically. "Draft a difficult message to a long-tenured employee about a role change" gets AI's full usefulness with none of the exposure. Teach the swap explicitly; it converts the line from a productivity tax into a technique.
</Callout>

## Make it hold: the three moves

A standard on paper is not a standard. Three moves make it real.

**Name it and date it.** One page, three tiers, examples from your actual work, an owner, and a review date. Send it from the leader, not from a system announcement. The signature matters: it tells the team this is a leadership standard, not an IT formality.

**Provide the sanctioned path.** Here is the part most organizations get backwards: the data line fails when it is only a wall. Your people are pasting things into AI tools because the tools genuinely help. If the compliant path is "do not," the actual path becomes "do not get caught." Approve a real tool, on business terms, and the standard suddenly costs nothing to follow. Prohibition without provision is how shadow adoption becomes permanent.

**Ask, do not audit.** In the next team conversation: "what are you all using AI for, honestly? Nobody is in trouble; I need to know so we can make it safe." The amnesty framing surfaces in one meeting what an audit would miss for a year, and it converts your team from the risk into the sensor network.

## The line is a floor, not the ceiling

Draw the data line and you have handled the sharpest risk, but notice what you have really built: the first piece of a personal governing standard for AI in your organization. The same discipline extends upward: [which tasks get delegated to these tools at all, and which never do](/awakening/what-to-delegate-to-ai-and-what-to-never); what gets verified before it ships; who answers for AI-assisted work. Data is simply the layer where the stakes are least forgiving, which is why it comes first.

Inside the AI Stakeholder Challenge, this discipline is part of the working fluency built across the seven days: using AI effectively, efficiently, ethically, and safely, with safety treated as a leadership standard rather than fine print. But the data line itself needs no program and no committee. One page, three tiers, your signature, this week. The twenty-minute shortcuts are happening today either way. The only question is whether they happen inside a standard you set.
