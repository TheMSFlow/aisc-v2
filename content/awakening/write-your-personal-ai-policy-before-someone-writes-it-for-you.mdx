At some point in the last year, you made a rule for yourself about AI. Maybe you decided you would never let it write something a specific person would receive as if it came from you. Maybe you decided the opposite. Either way, you probably never wrote it down, and if you are like most leaders, you could not state today, in plain sentences, what your own rules actually are.

That gap matters more than it seems. Your organization will eventually have an AI policy; committees are drafting them everywhere. But organizational policy answers only one question: what are people forbidden to do? It says nothing about the question that defines you as a leader: what do you choose to do, and refuse to do, even when no rule would stop you and nobody is watching? Leaders who have not examined their own AI standards are operating on assumptions. This guide converts the assumptions into principles, on one page, this week.

## Why write it down at all

Three reasons, each practical rather than ceremonial.

First, unwritten standards drift. Every busy week bends them a little: the shortcut becomes the habit, the exception becomes the norm, and six months later your actual practice is something you never decided. A written standard is a fixed point you can measure the drift against.

Second, you are already being watched for one. Your team calibrates their own AI behavior to what they observe of yours, not to the compliance memo. The leader who uses these tools deliberately, with visible standards, licenses deliberateness; the leader who is erratic licenses everything. Whether you wrote it or not, [your practice is already your team's real policy](/awakening/ai-governance-the-risk-is-already-inside).

Third, the writing is the examination. Most leaders discover their actual standards only in the attempt to state them, and the discoveries are the point: the line you thought you held but cannot define, the rule you have been breaking comfortably, the conviction you did not know you had until you saw it in your own sentence.

## The six standards

Write in first person, in plain language, and write the standard you will actually keep. A standard you cannot maintain is not a standard; it is a wish. Six sections cover the dimensions that matter.

**1. My delegation standard.** Which categories of work I hand to AI, under what conditions, and which I keep permanently regardless of how capable the tools become. The strong version names both sides: "I will use AI for work that is structured and repeatable. I will not use it for anything involving the wellbeing, assessment, or career of a specific person; those conversations are mine." If you have not drawn this line yet, [the delegation-line briefing](/awakening/what-to-delegate-to-ai-and-what-to-never) is the working discipline for it.

**2. My verification standard.** What I check before AI-assisted work leaves my hands. The strong version is uncomfortable and short: every fact, every number, every name, every time it will inform a decision or reach another person. Include the accountability sentence: "whatever ships under my name is mine, however it was produced."

**3. My data standard.** What I will never put into an AI tool: other people's confidential information, unannounced decisions, anything whose disclosure to a stranger would be an incident. Write the tiers explicitly, because in the moment, deadline pressure will argue for the exception.

**4. My disclosure standard.** When I tell people AI was involved in what they are receiving. This one has no universal answer, which is exactly why it needs your answer: the recommendation letter, the eulogy paragraph, the performance summary, the message of condolence. Decide where authenticity requires the work to be witnessed as yours, and where assistance is simply modern work.

**5. My development standard.** What I owe the people I lead: whether I hoard the skill or build it in others, what I model publicly, how I respond when someone on my team uses these tools badly, or brilliantly. Leadership has always included building the next generation of capability; state your share of it.

**6. My review standard.** When I revisit this document. The technology moves; a policy with no review date is a snapshot, not a standard. Quarterly is right for most leaders. Put the date on the page.

> Rules you inherit tell people what you comply with. Standards you author tell people who you are.

## Make it real in three moves

**Write it in one sitting.** Ninety minutes, six sections, imperfect. A rough page you actually hold beats a polished one you never finish. This is a private document; you are not writing it for an audience, which is precisely what makes it honest.

**Test it against last month.** Read your six standards against your actual recent behavior. Every mismatch is information: either the behavior needs to change or the standard was aspirational costume. Adjust whichever was wrong. This step is where the document earns its keep.

**Let it leak, selectively.** The document is private, but its effects should not be. Mention a standard when it becomes relevant: "I do not use AI for messages like this one, so this took me a while, and I meant it." Watch what that does in a room. Authored standards, stated without preaching, are among the most quietly authoritative moves available to a leader, and for an emerging leader especially, they signal something titles cannot: this person has done their own thinking.

## The page is a beginning

A personal AI policy is the smallest complete artifact of AI leadership: judgment, examined and committed to paper. It is also, not coincidentally, one of the closing documents of the AI Stakeholder Challenge's six-month roadmap, where it sits alongside the declared territory and the capability work as part of a leader's full operating position. The challenge builds the clarity and fluency that make the six standards easy to write, because by then they are descriptions of practice rather than aspirations.

But nothing about the page requires waiting. Ninety minutes, this week, first person, plain language. Someone is going to define how you use AI. The only question is whether it is a committee, your habits, or you, on purpose.
