The most dangerous property of modern AI is not that it makes mistakes. Your team makes mistakes. You make mistakes. The danger is the delivery: AI produces its errors in the same fluent, structured, confident voice as its accuracies. There is no hesitation to notice, no "I think" to catch, none of the tells that a decade of managing people has trained you to read. The wrong number arrives wearing the same suit as the right one.

Every leader working with AI eventually learns this. The only question is whether they learn it from a briefing like this one or from an incident with their name on it: the confident figure that was fabricated, the plausible citation that does not exist, the summary that quietly inverted the one point that mattered. This guide is the checklist that replaces the incident.

## Discernment is the second half of the skill

Working with AI has two halves. The first is direction: briefing the tool with role, context, and deliverable so the output is worth evaluating at all. The second half is discernment: what you do when the output comes back, before it goes anywhere.

Most people skip the second half for an understandable reason: the output looks finished. It is formatted, articulate, and instant, and everything about its presentation whispers "done." Treat that whisper as noise. An AI output is not a finished product; it is a draft from a fast, capable collaborator who does not know your world and cannot tell you when it is unsure. Four questions stand between that draft and your signature.

## Question one: is it actually right?

The factual gate, and it is non-negotiable for anything that will inform a decision or leave your hands. Every fact, figure, name, date, and quotation gets verified against a source you trust. Not because most of them will be wrong; most will be right, which is precisely what makes the wrong ones lethal. Nine verified claims build the trust that lets the tenth, fabricated one, sail through.

The working rules: numbers get checked against the underlying data, every time. Citations and references get confirmed to exist, because AI invents plausible-sounding sources with special enthusiasm. And anything surprising, anything that would change what you do if true, gets independent confirmation before it changes what you do. In [a finance seat or anywhere a board relies on your numbers](/awakening/the-briefing-your-board-expects-you-to-have-had), this single habit is the difference between AI making you faster and AI making you briefly famous.

## Question two: is it right for here?

Correct in general is not correct for you. AI produces the world's average answer: the advice that is usually sound, the framing that usually fits, the plan that would work at a typical organization. You do not lead a typical organization; you lead yours, with its history, its constraints, its politics, and its actual customers.

So interrogate the fit: does this reflect how things really work here, or how they work on average? What context did the tool not have, because you did not give it or could not? What would a colleague with twenty years in your world wince at? The output that survives this question is rarely the one that arrived; it is the one you reshaped. That reshaping is not overhead on the process. It is the process, and it is where your value enters the work.

## Question three: what is missing?

Fluent output has a hypnotic property: it fills the frame, so the frame looks complete. The sharpest discernment question is about what is not on the page. Which stakeholder's perspective is absent? Which risk went unmentioned? Which option was never generated because your brief accidentally excluded it, or because the average of the world's writing never considers it?

The practical move: ask the tool itself to argue the other side. "What are the three strongest objections to this recommendation?" "What would a skeptical reviewer flag?" AI is genuinely useful for attacking its own output, but only if you ask, because it volunteers nothing. The leader who asks is doing something recognizable: it is the same move as asking a strong deputy "what am I not seeing?", applied to a collaborator who must be prompted to be honest about its blind spots.

## Question four: whose judgment does this need before it moves?

The routing question. Some outputs are yours to clear: the internal draft, the analysis you will present and defend. Some need a domain expert's eye before they move: the legal language, the compliance-adjacent claim, the technical specification you can brief but not evaluate. And some, [the ones sitting on the judgment side of the delegation line](/awakening/what-to-delegate-to-ai-and-what-to-never), should not have been delegated at all, and the checklist is where you catch that this one got through.

The rule underneath: accountability does not transfer to the tool. Whatever ships, shipped from you. "The AI produced it" has exactly the standing of "my intern wrote it": true, and irrelevant to whose name is on the outcome.

> Verification is not distrust of the tool. It is respect for your own signature.

<Callout label="The sixty-second version">
Right? Right for here? What is missing? Who else needs to see it? For routine, low-stakes outputs the four questions take a minute, and the minute is the habit. Reserve the full treatment, sourcing, counter-arguments, expert review, for anything that informs a real decision or leaves the building. Calibrating the depth to the stakes is itself the judgment being practiced.
</Callout>

## The habit is the asset

Run the checklist for a month and something better than safety happens: you develop a feel for where AI fails in your specific domain: which tasks it handles cleanly, where it drifts, what its confident wrongness smells like in your field. That calibrated instinct is the real skill, and it cannot be borrowed or bought; it is built from reps of directed output and honest evaluation.

This loop, brief well, then discern deliberately, is the core practice of the AI Stakeholder Challenge's fluency work: using AI effectively, efficiently, ethically, and safely on your own real tasks, with the discernment step treated as the leadership act it is. Start tonight, on the next output you were about to accept. Four questions, one minute, your name protected. The suit the wrong answer wears stops working on you the day you start looking underneath it.
