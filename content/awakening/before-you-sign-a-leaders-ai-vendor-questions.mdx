The demo was impressive. It always is: the demo is the one part of an AI product that has been rehearsed hundreds of times, on data chosen because it performs. Somewhere between that demo and your signature, somebody in your organization is supposed to figure out whether the product is real, safe, and worth the money, and in most organizations that somebody defaults to whoever seems most technical.

That default is a mistake, and it costs real money. Not because technical evaluation is worthless, but because the questions that actually kill bad AI deals are not technical. They are leadership questions: about data, accountability, dependency, and proof. You are more qualified to ask them than your most technical hire, because you are the one whose name is on the outcome. Here is the list.

## The data questions: what happens to what we put in

Ask first, because the answers disqualify more vendors than anything else.

**"What happens to the data we put into this?"** You are listening for a specific, boring answer: where it is stored, who can access it, whether it is used to improve the product for other customers, and what happens to it when you leave. Vague reassurance ("we take security very seriously") is a red flag precisely because the real answer is easy for a serious vendor: it is in their contract. In [a clinical setting or anywhere confidentiality is the product](/awakening/ai-governance-the-risk-is-already-inside), this question alone should gate the meeting.

**"Can you delete everything of ours, provably, on request?"** The answer reveals whether they actually control their own systems.

**"Does our data train anything that serves other customers?"** Some products improve themselves on customer data. That may be acceptable; it must be known and chosen, never discovered.

## The accountability questions: when it is wrong

Every AI product is sometimes wrong. Serious vendors know exactly how theirs fails; weak ones are offended by the question.

**"What does this product get wrong, and how often?"** The only wrong answer is "it doesn't really." Ask for their measured error rates on work like yours, not their accuracy marketing. A vendor who cannot describe their product's failure modes has either not measured them or will not say; both should end the conversation.

**"How would we know when it is wrong?"** Does the product signal uncertainty, or does it deliver every answer with equal confidence? If your team cannot tell strong outputs from weak ones, every output inherits the review burden of the weakest, which quietly deletes the promised time savings.

**"Who is accountable when an error reaches our customer?"** Read the contract's liability language before believing any verbal answer. Most AI contracts assign the vendor's responsibility a ceiling and your exposure none. That may be a deal you accept knowingly; the operative word is knowingly, because the accountability for AI-assisted work [never actually transfers to a tool or its maker](/awakening/what-to-delegate-to-ai-and-what-to-never). It stays with whoever ships the work.

## The dependency questions: the exit you sign along with the entrance

**"What does leaving look like?"** Say it plainly in the room: walk me through the day we stop using you. Where is our data, in what format, what breaks in our workflow, what have our people forgotten how to do? Products that embed deeply are valuable and dangerous for the same reason. Price the exit before the entrance, because the vendor certainly has.

**"What happens to our workflow when you change the product?"** AI products change under their customers constantly: models get swapped, behavior shifts, features vanish into higher pricing tiers. Ask what notice you get, what stays stable, and what their track record of breaking changes looks like. You are buying a moving target; know how fast it moves.

**"What does this cost at real scale?"** The pilot price and the deployed price are different products. Model the invoice at full adoption, including the seats, the usage, and the tier they will inevitably suggest next year.

> The demo shows you the product on its best day, with someone else's data. Every question on this list is about its worst day, with yours.

## The proof question: the only evidence that counts

**"Give us a reference customer our size, in our industry, one year in."** Then actually make the call, and ask the reference the unglamorous questions: what surprised you, what does it get wrong, would you buy it again, what do you wish you had negotiated. One honest hour with a year-in customer outweighs every slide in the deck. A vendor who cannot produce such a customer is asking you to be one, which is a different price than the one on the invoice, and sometimes worth paying, but only on purpose, with a pilot scoped to prove the thing you most doubt.

<Callout label="The pilot that actually proves something">
If you trial the product, define before day one: the specific friction it must reduce, the number that will measure it, who owns the trial, and the date a decision gets made. A pilot without those four is not an evaluation; it is a free extension of the sales process, and its ending was written before it began.
</Callout>

## Why this is your job, not procurement's

Underneath every question here is a single leadership fact: an AI product is not software you install. It is judgment you are inviting into your organization's work, and the vendor's standards, about accuracy, about data, about accountability, become embedded in your operations the day it deploys. Choosing whose judgment gets that access was never a technical decision. It is a governance decision wearing a procurement costume.

Which means the real preparation for the vendor meeting is not reading their documentation. It is knowing your own position: what you will delegate to machines and what you will never, where your data lines sit, and what ground you are actually trying to claim, because a leader who knows those things can evaluate any product in an hour, and a leader who does not can be sold anything.
