There are two ways a leader starts with AI, and you can tell them apart within a month.

The first leader collects tools. A subscription here, an app their nephew recommended there, a folder of bookmarked demos. Six months later they are busier than before, mildly more efficient at tasks that never mattered, and no more clear about what any of it means for their world. They started with the question "what can AI do?", which is the tool question, and it has no bottom.

The second leader starts with a different question: "where, in my specific world, has AI opened ground that nobody has claimed yet?" That is the territory question. It has an answer, the answer is specific, and pursuing it changes a leader's position rather than just their pace.

This guide is a first week built around the second question. It asks for five working sessions, roughly an hour each, alone, with a document open. No purchases required.

## Day one: write down what you actually believe

Before AI can create value in your world, you need an honest baseline of what you currently think it is. Not what the keynote said. What you believe.

Open a blank page and answer three questions in plain sentences:

- What do I think AI actually is, in one paragraph a smart friend outside my industry would understand?
- What do I suspect it means for my organization, my audience, or my mission?
- What have I been pretending to understand in meetings?

The third question is the valuable one. Most leaders have been nodding along for a year or more, and the gap between public confidence and private clarity has quietly widened. Naming that gap on paper, where nobody else can see it, is the single fastest unlock in this entire week. If the exercise reveals more gap than you expected, [the board-position briefing](/awakening/the-briefing-your-board-expects-you-to-have-had) goes deeper on closing it.

## Day two: audit where your week actually goes

Territory requires capacity, and most leaders have none to spare. So before looking outward at opportunity, look at where your hours currently leak.

List everything you personally did last week that fits any of these:

- Work that follows a pattern you could describe to a competent assistant
- Drafting, summarizing, formatting, or reorganizing anything
- Answering questions you have answered before in nearly the same words

Estimate the hours honestly. Most leaders who run this exercise with structure find 10 to 20 hours a week sitting in that list: real hours going to work that AI could handle under their direction, while decisions only they can make wait at the edge of the calendar.

> The first thing AI should give any leader is not a capability. It is their mornings back.

You are not automating anything yet. You are establishing that the capacity for the territory you are about to name already exists inside your own week, mispriced.

One caution while the list is in front of you: do not act on it today. The instinct at this point is to grab a tool and start clearing the list, which is exactly the tool trap this week exists to avoid. Hours recovered with no destination get refilled by the same low-value work within a month; every leader who has deleted an afternoon of email only to grow a new one knows the pattern. The list keeps. The point of today is the number at the bottom of it. When the week is over and you are ready to act on the list, [the delegation-line briefing](/awakening/what-to-delegate-to-ai-and-what-to-never) is the discipline for deciding what to hand over and what to keep.

## Day three: map the ground that just opened

Now the outward look. In every world, AI has made some things abundant that used to be scarce, and that shift always opens ground. The pattern:

- **For a business owner**, abundance of analysis and drafting means the operational bottleneck that defined your margins may no longer be fixed. The territory might be [a service you could not afford to offer last year](/awakening/the-service-you-could-not-afford-to-offer-last-year).
- **For a creator**, abundance of content makes a trusted voice scarcer and more valuable. The territory is rarely "more output." It is becoming the signal your audience checks against the noise, including about AI itself.
- **For a nonprofit leader**, abundance of intelligence at low cost is the first genuine leverage the sector has been offered in decades. The territory might be the program your team of six could never staff, now within reach.

Write the equivalent sentence for your world: *"AI has just made ___ abundant, which makes ___ scarce and valuable, and nobody around me has claimed it."* Draft it badly, then redraft. The precision matters more than the polish.

<Callout label="Why nobody has claimed it">
The ground is usually open not because nobody sees it but because everyone assumes somebody more qualified is already moving. In most worlds, that somebody does not exist. The qualification being waited on is clarity, and clarity is buildable in days, not years.
</Callout>

## Day four: pressure-test it against reality

A territory is only real if it survives three tests. Run yesterday's sentence through each:

- **The specificity test.** Could you explain the territory to your team in two sentences without a single abstract word? "Digital transformation" fails. "Every donor report we send goes out in a week instead of a quarter, and we become the most transparent organization in our space" passes.
- **The authority test.** Does claiming this ground fit who you already are to your people? The best territories extend existing trust rather than requiring new credentials.
- **The regret test.** If someone else in your world claimed exactly this ground twelve months from now, would it genuinely cost you? If not, keep looking. Territory you would not miss is territory you do not want.

If your sentence fails a test, that is the process working. Redraft and rerun. Two or three passes usually land somewhere solid, and the failed drafts teach you as much as the final one.

## Day five: decide what kind of week this was

Look at the four documents in front of you: what you believe, where your hours go, the ground you named, and how it held up under pressure. Then make one honest assessment.

If the territory came out sharp and you can already see the first ninety days, move. Put a weekly session on your calendar to advance it, and treat [governance as your very next stop](/awakening/ai-governance-the-risk-is-already-inside), because ground claimed without standards is ground you can lose in one incident.

If instead the week produced something blurrier, a real leak in your hours, a territory you half-believe, a nagging sense that your understanding is still borrowed, then what you have found is not failure. You have found exactly what a structured, guided process is for. Working alone gets a leader to the edge of this. The 7-day AI Stakeholder Challenge exists for the distance past it: clarity you build rather than borrow, real hours recovered and counted, and a territory declared with a 6-month roadmap behind it and other leaders around it.

Either way, you are done collecting tools. That question has no bottom. The territory question does, and this week you touched it.
