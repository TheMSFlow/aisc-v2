# The Awakening — Research Guide
> Read `agent-guides/AISC_BRIEF.md` and `agent-guides/blog/BLOG_BRIEF.md` first.
> This guide governs every factual claim that appears in a blog article. The Awakening sells judgment to leaders. One invented number destroys the exact thing the blog exists to build.

---

## The Standard

Every claim in an article is one of three things, and you must know which before you write it:

| Kind | Test | How it appears in copy |
|------|------|------------------------|
| Verified fact | You found it in a primary source you can name | Stated plainly, attributed when the source adds weight |
| Reported claim | A credible outlet or study says it; you could not verify the underlying data | Attributed explicitly: "McKinsey's 2025 survey reports..." |
| AISC position | A stance from the brief, the program, or Michael Steve's frameworks | Owned confidently as a position, never dressed up as external data |

Anything that fits none of the three does not go in the article.

## Hard Rules

- **Never invent statistics.** No approximated percentages, no "studies show" without a study, no plausible-sounding numbers. If you cannot source it, restructure the sentence to work without it. A strong unnumbered sentence beats a fabricated numbered one.
- **Date-stamp AI capability claims.** What AI can and cannot do changes fast. Any capability claim is written so it stays true as models improve, or it carries its date: "as of mid-2026." Never write capability ceilings ("AI cannot yet...") without a date anchor.
- **Prefer primary sources.** The regulation itself over coverage of it. The published survey over the article citing it. The company's announcement over commentary on it.
- **Distinguish adoption from impact.** "Most employees use AI at work" and "AI improves their output" are different claims with different evidence. Keep them separate.
- **Mind the knowledge cutoff.** If you are writing about events, regulation, or model capabilities and cannot verify current state, either verify with live search or write the claim at a level of generality that does not depend on recency.
- **External links are endorsements.** Link only to authoritative sources: primary documents, major research institutions, regulators, established publications. Never link to competitors of AISC or to AI tool marketing pages.

## AISC Internal Source Material

For program facts, frameworks, and positioning, these are canonical and require no external sourcing:

| Source | Use for |
|--------|---------|
| `agent-guides/AISC_BRIEF.md` | Program structure, pillars, audiences, reframes, pricing, the Roadmap system |
| `agent-guides/COPY_GUIDE.md` § Numbers to Know | Every AISC number: days, sessions, seats, prices |
| `website-context/aisc_resources/` | Day-by-day program depth: documents, worksheets, session notes |
| `website-context/learning center/` | What participants actually see and use inside the challenge |

`website-context/` is read-only reference. Quote its ideas, never copy its text verbatim into articles.

**Program facts must match the brief exactly.** 7 days, 4 live sessions, the Day 7 governance capstone, tier pricing: get one wrong in public and it undermines the sales page that says otherwise. When an article states a program fact, check it against `COPY_GUIDE.md` § Numbers to Know in the same session.

## Research Output

When research is a separate task from writing, deliver findings as a claims table the writer can drop into the workflow:

```
| Claim | Kind (verified / reported / position) | Source | Date | Suggested phrasing |
```

Include the failed searches too: claims you tried to verify and could not, so the writer knows what NOT to assert.
